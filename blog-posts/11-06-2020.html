Blog Post - November 6th, 2020

This week I read the article "Designing for accessibility is not that hard", written by Pablo Stanely for UX Collective.

Stanely defines digital accessibility as "the practice of building digital content and applications that can be used by a wide range of people, including individuals who have visual, motor, auditory, speech, or cognitive disabilities."

According to Stanley, approximately 20% of Americans have disabilities. Furthermore, in 2017, there were 814 lawsuits related to website accessibility. As such, digital accessibility is an essential component of good website design.

Stanley lists 8 guidelines that developers should follow to assure digital accessibility:

  1. Add enough color contrast
  2. Don't use color alone to make critical information understandable
  3. Design usable focus states
  4. Use labels or instructions with form fields and inputs
  5. Write useful alternative text for your images and other non-text content
  6. Use correct markup on your content
  7. Support keyboard navigation
  8. Think beyond

Add enough color contrast

In the pursuit of cool looking color palettes, developers often overlook color contrast. People with low vision have difficulty reading webpages that have low contrast. According to W3C, the contrast ratio between the text and background colors should be at least 4.5 to 1. As the text size becomes larger, this ratio becomes smaller.

We can use the Luminosity Colour Contrast Ratio Analyser to analyze text and background color contrast.

Consider the following styling:

.low-contrast {
   color: #e61082;
   background-color: #a903fc;
}
.high-contrast {
   color: #a81164;
   background-color: #d5c8db;
}

and this content:

<p class="low-contrast">
   IMPORTANT ANNOUNCEMENT: Please contact your supervisor immediately!
</p>
<p class="high-contrast">
   IMPORTANT ANNOUNCEMENT: Please contact your supervisor immediately!
</p>

The low-contrast paragraph looks like:

IMPORTANT ANNOUNCEMENT: Please contact your supervisor immediately!

It's contrast ratio is: 1.16:1. This is very low and difficult to read, even for a person with high vision.

The high-contrast paragraph looks like:

IMPORTANT ANNOUNCEMENT: Please contact your supervisor immediately!

It's contrast ratio is: 4.48:1. This is more than adequate and much easier to read.

Don't use color alone to make critical information understandable

Some people have difficulty differentiating between colors. As such, if information is conveyed exclusively through color, those people will have difficulty understanding it.

Developers have a myriad of non-color options to help convey information, including but not limited to: text labels, patterns, icons, titles, font weight, text styles, shapes, size, and textures.

Try viewing a webpage in black and white to see if a person with low vision could read it.

Design usable focus states

Focus indicators are style changes and other visual indicators that the user is interacting with an element. For example, when selecting an input field on a form, a border often appears around the element to let the user know that they are typing in that field.

Links, form fields, widgets, buttons, and menu items should all be focusable and have focus indicators.

Use labels or instructions with form fields and inputs

Developers should not use placeholders as labels for fields. These disappear when the user begins inputing data, and the text is often low contrast. In addition, screen readers often utilize labels to navigate a webpage, and nonlabeled text may be skipped over.

Instead, developers should use labels to assert that users always have context.

Write useful alternative text for your images and other non-text content

People with low vision need useful alterntive text to understand non-text content. Developers should always include the "alt" attribute on non-text content.

Use correct markup on your content

Correct markup helps everyone. Readers can infer titles, sections, and structure. Users with screen readers can navigate the webpage with ease.

Proper markup structure assures that the browser creates a correct accessibility tree.

Support keyboard navigation

Not every user has a mouse. Many people navigate the web using exclusively keyboards and screen readers.

Interactable elements should be navigable in a logical and intuitive way via the keyboard, primarily through use of the tab and enter keys.

Think beyond

Think longer term. Get your products accessed by an accessibility auditor, have an accessibility expert on hand, and make accessibility an essential component of your design.
